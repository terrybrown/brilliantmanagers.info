The guide covers a lot of ground. But reading it isn't the point — understanding where you actually stand is.

This section is about measurement: how to score yourself honestly across every area in the framework, what to do with those scores, and how to turn a snapshot of your current state into a plan for getting better. Work through it before you dive into the chapters, or return to it once you've read enough to have a clearer picture of your gaps.

## The Five Levels

Rather than a numeric scale, the framework uses five named competency levels. Each name carries its own meaning — no translation required.

| Level | What it means |
|---|---|
| <ScoringBadge level="Needs Improvement" /> | You're not yet demonstrating this consistently. The skill is underdeveloped or rarely applied. This is a starting point, not a judgement. |
| <ScoringBadge level="Basic" /> | You show some understanding and practice, but it's inconsistent or has limited impact. You've started — it's not yet a strength. |
| <ScoringBadge level="Proficient" /> | You meet expectations and demonstrate this reliably. Others can count on you here. A solid, healthy place for most skills. |
| <ScoringBadge level="Advanced" /> | You go beyond expectations with strong impact. Others often rely on your strength in this area; you informally coach others. |
| <ScoringBadge level="Expert" /> | You set the standard. You influence others through mastery and actively develop this skill in the people around you. Should be rare — claim this only with real evidence. |

## How to Score Yourself

Go through each skill in the guide and ask: which level genuinely describes where I am right now — not where I want to be, not where I was at my best?

**Some anchor examples:**

- If your empathy is consistently strong, people comment on it, and you're known for it — you're likely **Advanced** or **Expert**.
- If you struggle with challenge and conflict, avoid raising opposing views, and go along with the status quo — you're likely **Needs Improvement** or **Basic**.
- If you engage with strategy and vision but don't yet feel confident creating a strong outcome yourself — you're likely **Basic** or **Proficient**.

Most people land at **Proficient** on the things they're naturally good at, and **Basic** or **Needs Improvement** on the things they've avoided. That's normal. The value is in seeing the full picture honestly.

## Getting Other Perspectives

Self-assessment has well-documented limits. Two biases work against you here: [imposter syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impostor_syndrome) tends to make people underrate themselves, while [Dunning-Kruger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dunning%E2%80%93Kruger_effect) can lead to overconfidence in areas you know least. The [Johari window](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johari_window) model captures this well — there are things others see in you that you don't see in yourself, and vice versa.

Where possible, supplement your self-assessment with:

- **Manager input** — what does your manager see as your strongest areas, and where do they think you should focus?
- **Peer and team feedback** — the people you work closest with day-to-day often have the clearest view of how your skills show up in practice.
- **Role requirements** — some skills matter more in your specific context. A manager in a research org needs different things than one leading a sales team. Weight accordingly.

## What to Do With Your Scores

The goal isn't a good score. It's an accurate one.

Once you have a complete picture, look for the pattern: are there areas where multiple skills cluster at **Needs Improvement** or **Basic**? That's where the real leverage is — not in polishing the things you're already **Advanced** at.

Use the scores to start a conversation with your manager. The scorecard works best as a shared artefact, not a private one.
